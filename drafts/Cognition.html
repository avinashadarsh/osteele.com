Separate Selves

I’m more similar to other people my age than to my earlier self.  There’s an illusion of continuity because I have the same memories (or memories of memories), similar skills and deficits (good analytic capability, poor fine-motor coordination), and because the changes in my personality were gradual, but there’s no reason for me to think of my current self as the same person as my twenty-year-old self, rather than, say, the same self as my wife or colleagues.

What are the ethical implications of this?

Maps and Directions

Directions have two forms: a list of instructions, and a visual representation.  I can’t use the first.  When I get a list of directions, I have to turn it into a map.  My wife can’t use the second.  When she gets a map with a route on it, she has to translate it into a series of “go straight three miles”, “turn left on elliot st.”.  I can’t remember these.

Decisions

I’ve noticed two different attitudes towards decision-making.  Some people (such as myself) view decisions as transitions.  Before you make the decision, the future looks one way (or maybe it’s indeterminate).  Afterwards, it looks another way.  The point is to navigate the decision as quickly as possible, so that you can get to work on refining the post-decision world, and accommodating to it.

Others (such as my wife) view decision-making as a process.

In my experience there are two attitudes towards time.  A clock-driven approach. An event-driven approach chooses actions by the (non-temporal) context and situation.  Both of these are viable approaches to the world, but they don’t mix well.  Clock-driven people tend to appear inflexible; event-driven people tend to be late.  When they try to coordinate, the difference in goals causes friction and dissent.
