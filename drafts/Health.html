Getting into getting into shape

Preparing to get into shape.  There’s some setup work to do first.  The point is to turn your body into one that can expend a lot of calories.  This means putting enough muscle onto it that you can exercise (muscle also increases your resting metabolism, which means your burning calories even when you don’t exercise), raising your cardiovascular efficiency, and increasing your lactic tolerance.  You could optimize for: burning calories, raising your cardiovascular efficiency, or increasing your lactic acid tolerance.  As anyone who has done engineering knows, if there are multiple factors you can’t optimize for all of them at once.

The most important principle is not to injure yourself.  My rule of thumb is to only do a little more than I did the day before.  My other rule of thumb is that if anything feels weird, sore, or damaged, to stop instantly, and not exercise that body part until a few days after it has recovered completely.  Getting injured is like getting stopped for speeding.  The time you lose more than wipes out any gains you made, as well as having its own penalties.

At any one time there’s a long pole.  Either you get sore, or you get winded, or you get cramps, or there just isn’t enough time for a workout.  Identify the long pole, and work on that one; don’t worry about the other factors until one of them becomes the long pole:

Sore muscles.  Wait until they heal.  Learn to identify the difference between DOMS and injury.  Actually, if you wait until you’re not sore you don’t need to.  If it’s DOMS it will take a day (if it’s a small muscle such as your abdominals, or if you’re very young), or two days (if you’re in your twenties or early thirties), or three days (if you’re forty – I don’t now anything past her), or five days (if it’s the first time in your life that you’ve ever exercised a muscle).  You can get soreness from weight or from duration.  When I started running I got sore.  Each time I ran faster I got sore again.

Out of breath.  This is cardiovascular fitness.  What your body needs to do is increase your heart size, expand your circulatory system, etc.  But you don’t need to know this.  What you need to do is spend as much time as you can going as fast as you can.  The way to do this is interval training.  After you warm up, run (if running is your exercise) as fast as you can for a minute, and then drop your speed, and repeat.  You’ll be able to go faster and longer that way than if you try to do it all at one burst.  When I’m running on a treadmill, I run at the following speeds (in mph, each for one minute): 6,7,8,9,9; 6,7,8,8,9; 6,7,7,8,9; 6,6,7,8,9.  When I’m in peak shape, I add a minute to each of these.  When I’m in the best shape I’ve ever been, I add two minutes.  (11 mph feels like the fastest a human can go, and it’s all I can do to sustain a minute.  Then I remind myself that I’d have to run that fast for 2 1/3 hours to qualify for a marathon***, and that i’d have to run 4mp faster (x%) to run a 4m miles.

Muscle cramps.  This is the opposite of cardiovascular.  You want a sustained pace

But all this is just to tune up to the point where you can exercise.  Once you’re tuned up, you won’t get sore muscles, or cramps, or out of breath.

“Yes pain no gain.”  Exercise might be uncomfortable, but not painful – at least, not while you’re doing it.

Calorie Accounting

Here’s a few tips on weight maintenance.  The high bit is that calorie expenditure needs to exceed calorie intake.  You can work with either side of the equation: increasing calorie expenditure (mostly exercise, but there’s a few tricks), and decreasing calorie intake (”dieting”, but the word implies willpower, which isn’t the most effective dieting technique for most of us).

Back Out

Or, a Sharp Pain in the Ass

In fall of 2001, my back went out.  For a while, I had sciatica (a pain in the leg that felt like a live wire had been implanted in the muscle), and then my disk ruptured on one side, and then on the other, and then I underwent a four-hour operation to remove the material that was impinging on the nerves projecting from my spine.

Now I’m in better shape than I was then. In fact, in most ways I’m in better shape than I was in my twenties.  Different, but better.  (My disk will never be the same, but for the first time in my life I have strong enough torso muscles and good enough posture to protect them.)  There’s nothing like the threat, and memory, of unbearable pain to make you take care of your body.
