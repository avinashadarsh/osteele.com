Beauty and Brains

I used to believe it was unfair to favor beautiful people.  They were just lucky to be beautiful; luck isn’t fair; and it would compound the unfairness to favor the lucky.

At the same time, I thought it was fine to favor smart people.

Notice the contradiction here?

One assumption was that you didn’t have to work to be beautiful.  But you do.  Some of beauty is bone structure – you can’t change that.  Some of it is complexion.  Cf. Paul Graham’s essay about being popular: it takes a lot of work.  Beauty takes a lot of work too.

So why not reward it?

One answer is that, unlike being smart, being beautiful isn’t functional.  But seeming smart isn’t functional either.  Although the same raw ingredients can make you seem smart and make you useful too, there’s a lot of work in one that isn’t in the other.  (Robert De Niro said that he chose between being a personality and being an actor, because he didn’t think he could do both.)  Also, intelligence helps in being attractive too, just like it helps in almost anything.

Many beautiful actresses aren’t that physically attractive, or wouldn’t be if someone else’s brain were living in them.  (I can’t speak about beautiful actors.  I just don’t pay enough attention.  Although I bet that Brad Pitt spends a lot of time working on his body.)  Part of it is that, like athletes, they take care of their body.  So don’t compare an actress to the population at large; compare an actress to people you see jogging or at the gym.  Part of it is that they act beautiful.  John Travolta said about Saturday Night Live that he didn’t actually know how to dance; he just acted dancing.  (At first I thought this was ridiculous.  It’s like saying you don’t really know how to pass a standardized test, but you passed it anyway by acting knowledgeable.  But maybe it’s more like acting like you know French.  I don’t know French; if you’ve practiced for a while, you can probably fool me.)  Actresses act beautiful.
